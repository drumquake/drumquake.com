LEARNING TO PLAY THE DRUMS BY EAR

2016/04/28
"There's nothing wrong with copying. Eventually, you'll create your own style anyway". —Dave Weckl

Practicing rudiments gets tedious. We naturally want to learn things we're exited about. Why not also try to copy our favorite drummers, then?

The break at the beginning of this track is an old artifact that I copied from Mikkey Dee decades ago:

The opening break on this video is the original:

https://youtu.be/TJH0eBtnbcs


You'd never guess it! It's one of many ideas that I've integrated into my playing over the years.

BENEFITS

Most of what great drummers do lies in how they play, not what they play. When you read charts, you miss out on all that can't be written—punch, tone, swing.

You'll also be confronted with new challenges: how do they make it sound like that? Is it the way it's mixed? Why did they choose to play what they did, instead of something else? How does what they play relate to the other musicians?

EXERCISE

Listen, over and over, to the part you want to learn. You may want to use transcription.

Next, practice only one limb at the time; then two and two limbs; three and three; until you're ready to practice all four. Practice them as you would 360 paradiddles.

When you know how to play it, listen again and see if you can also copy the drummer's style, sound, and feel.
